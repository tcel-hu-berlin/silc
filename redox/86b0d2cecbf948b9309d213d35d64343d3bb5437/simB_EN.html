Electrochemical cells are referred to as galvanic if they involve a spontaneous redox reaction that produces electricity. A galvanic cell consists of a cathodic half-cell, in which the reduction half-reaction occurs, and an anodic half-cell, in which the oxidative half-reaction occurs. Each half-cell contains a redox pair (e.g. Cu2+/Cu(s) or H+/H2), which has a corresponding standard reduction potential (see Help Menu). The electrons given out during the oxidation reaction flow through an electrical cicrcuit from the anode to the cathode. The two half-cells are also connected by a salt bridge, in which ions move between the anode and cathode to ensure the balance of charge.

Important: the connectivity of the voltmeter has no influence on the roles of cathode and anode! The redox pair with the highest reduction potential will always undergo reduction and act as the cathode A positive cell voltage simply means that the "+" side of the voltmeter is connected with the cathode, while a negative cell voltage means that the "+" side of the voltmeter is connected with the anode.

Galvanic cells can also be represented with an abbreviated (line) notation, such as at the bottom of Simulation B. Some key elements to note about the line cell notation are:

  • Single lines (|) represent boundaries between phases, e.g. between two solid phases or between a solid and a liquid.
  • Double lines (||) are used to denote the salt bridge.
  • Concentrations of solutions and partial pressures of gases, if known, are also commonly included.

Notes:

  • The + and - signs of the two sides should NOT be interpreted as a positive and negative charges! The charge of each electrode in a galvanic cell is determined by the chemistry taking place at the electrode-solution interface.
  • The pressures of all gasses are assumed to be 1 atm. The concentrations of all solutions are assumed to be 1 M.
  • Water molecules are not included in the submicroscopic visualization.
