Simulation C: Electrolytic cells

„Crash course” on electrolytic cells:Show

Unlike galvanic cells, electrolytic cells are characterized by an external power source that drives forward a normally non-spontaneous redox reaction. Here, the roles of anode and cathode are pre-determined by the power supply, i.e. one of the electrodes acquires a positive charge and acts as the anode, while the other electrode acquires a negative charge and acts as the cathode. The positive charge of the anode allows it to "extract" electrons from the species to be oxidized, while the negative charge of the cathode "delivers" electrons to the species that gets reduced. Salt bridges are not as common in electrolytic cells; if needed, porous membranes and other separation techniques are used to prevent interference between the anodic and cathodic reactions.

The goal of an electrolytic cell is generally to collect the products of the oxidation and/or the reduction. A common task when constructing an electrolytic cell is therefore to calculate the expected amount of products that will be generated. The following points are important to consider when making such calculations:

  • The quantity of electric current (I, measured in Amperes) determines how many coulombs of charge are passing a cross-section of the circuit at a given second (1 A = 1 C s-1).
  • The charge carried by an electron (elementary charge) is e = 1.6 × 10-19 C. It follows that a mole of electrons carries a charge of NA × e = 96,485 C, which is represented by the Faraday constant F.
  • The molar ratio between electrons and a particular product is determined by the corresponding redox reaction, which in most cases can be found in the the electrochemical series. For example, the oxidation of Cl- into chlorine gas produces two moles of electrons per mole of chlorine gas/
  • If the applied current and the duration of the electrolysis are known, the total amount of charge consumed and therefore the exact molar amount of a product can be determined using the above information.
  • For ideal gases, the volume of the produced gas can be calculated using the molar volume of 22.4 L mol-1 at standard temperature and pressure (STP). For solids, the mass of solid produced can be determined using its molar mass.

The application of the above points assumes that there are no energy losses or byproducts during the electrolysis.

Overview of the simulation:

  • The desired electrolytic cell can be selected from the drop-down menu "System."
  • The applied voltage and current can be varied using the buttons on the gray power supply.
  • Upon first clicking on START, the switch of the circuit will close. This will start the electrolysis with the selected voltage and current. The same button can be used to resume the simulation if it has been paused.
  • By clicking on PAUSE, you can "freeze time" in the simulation. You can use this feature to more carefully observe what is happening at specific time points during the electrolysis.
  • During the electrolysis, you can click on the magnifying glass symbols () to view animations of the behavior of electrons, ions, and molecules at the submicroscopic level.
  • The elapsed time and (where applicable) the collected amounts of relevant products since the start of the electrolysis are also shown. Note that time is displayed in minutes! The simulation runs much faster than real-time.
  • By clicking on the yellow button (↺), the system is reset and a new electrolysis can be set up.

Note:

  • If the applied voltage is insufficient, no electrolytic reaction will be observed! This simulation requires a particular threshold voltage to drive the reaction forward. In reality, the situation is a bit more continuous.
