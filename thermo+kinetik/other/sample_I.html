Example Lesson: Chemical Equilibrium

Natalia Spitha

Learning Goals

  • Describe the state of chemical equilibrium in terms of competing "forward" and "reverse" chemical reactions.
  • Define the term equilibrium constant.
  • Relate the mathematical definition of the equilibrium constant to amounts of molecules present at a submicroscopic level.
  • Optional: Name examples of reactions where the "equilibrium" is very one-sided (e.g. strong acid-strong base reactions)

Prior knowledge used

  • Ability to define the terms atom and ion.
  • Understanding of the notation used for chemical equations
  • Familiarity with the concept of mole, with molar concentration (in moles per liter), and with orders of magnitude (e.g. 1 μL = 10-6 L)

Helpful additional knowledge

  • Familiarity with the definition of acids as proton donors and bases as proton acceptors.
  • Ability to define the free energy change of a reaction (ΔG) and relate it to the spontaneity of the reaction.

Part 1: What is chemical equilibrium?

Let us consider the reaction between hydrogen sulfide (H2S) and cyanide (CN-). Both of these substances are quite toxic to humans, but, thankfully, they can also be useful for illustrating this next topic!

H2S + CN- ⟶ HS- + HCN

In this reaction, the hydrogen sulfide molecule donates a hydrogen ion (or proton) to the cyanide molecule:

This exchange occurs because hydrogen sulfide is slightly acidic, meaning that it has a tendency to donate protons. The negatively charged cyanide ion is a good proton "acceptor" and acts as a base.

The products of this reaction are hydrogen cyanide (HCN) molecule and hydrosulfide (HS-). However, this is not the end of the story! The HS- ion now has a negative charge, which makes it a good proton acceptor. Similarly, the hydrogen cyanide atom now has a proton that it can give away. As a result, the following reaction can also occur, converting our "products" back into the reactants!

Since the second reaction can also occur alongside the first one, it is more accurate to represent the equation not with a single right arrow (as above), but with a pair of opposite arrows:

H2S + CN- ⇌ HS- + HCN

It just so happens that hydrogen sulfide is a better acid (proton donor) than hydrogen cyanide (more on that on the Acids and Bases chapter!). This means that the first reaction (or forward reaction) is much more likely to happen than the second reaction (reverse reaction). But the reverse reaction will still occur, especially if there are many more HS- and HCN molecules available than H2S and CN- molecules.

The presence of two competing reactions affects how the original reaction progresses over time. At the beginning of the reaction, a lot of H2S and CN- is present, so the forward reaction occurs very frequently. As the molecules are consumed during the reaction, the rate of the forward reaction slows down over time.

As for the reverse reaction, it hardly occurs at the beginning of the reaction, both due to its unlikeliness and because of the very low amount of HS- and HCN available to react. However, as more and more HS- and HCN molecules are produced, the frequency (rate) of the reverse reaction rises.

Notice how the blue and red lines above converge to the same point at the end of the graph? This is not by chance; it is actually a key element of chemical reactions and it represents the transition of the reaction into chemical equilibrium! Eventually, as reactions progress, the rates of the forward and reverse reactions become the same.

Let's now view the same concept in terms of the amount of molecules present in the reaction as time progresses. The picture on the left shows the progress of the reaction on the "molecular level" (where each particle represents a μmol of particles), while the graph on the right shows the concentrations of H2S, CN-, HS-, and HCN in μmol/L over time. Here, it is better to talk about molar concentrations of molecules, since the closeness of the particles to each other is just as important as the number of particles itself.

As expected, the concentrations of the reactants (H2S and CN-) decrease over time, while the concentrations of the products increase. However, all species eventually stop changing in concentration. This is because, as shown before, the forward and reverse reactions of a system in equilibrium are happening at the same rate. Molecules still react with each other, but the two competing reactions "cancel each other out" in a way that makes the system not change overall.

So, how would you define the term chemical equilibrium? Let's summarize what we've learned so far:

  • Every time a (non-irreversible) reaction occurs, there is a competition between the forward, or "expected," direction of the reaction and the reverse reaction.
  • The rate of the reaction in each direction depends on the concentration of molecules that are present and available to react.
  • Chemical equilibrium is said to be reached when the forward and reverse reactions are happening at the same rate.
  • The reaction does not stop while at chemical equilibrium, as molecules continue to react with each other. However, there is no net change in the concentrations of the reactants and products once the system has reached equilibrium.
